- DeepXiv keywords: velocity distribution, binary disruption, supermassive black hole, loss cone dynamics, Galactic Centre remnants

**DeepXiv brief**

[research paper] The velocity distribution of observed hypervelocity stars (HVSs), particularly the absence of stars with velocities >700 km s⁻¹, contradicts predictions from binary disruption by the supermassive black hole (MBH) in the Galactic Centre under both full and empty loss cone regimes, even when accounting for multiple close encounters. This deficiency persists across secular instabilities and eccentric disk models, and no corresponding population of ejected stars is found in the Galactic Centre. While star-formation history tuning can marginally explain the velocity distribution, it fails to match observed HVS flight times; however, binary collisions with the MBH may produce observable remnants like G2-like dust clouds in the Galactic Centre.

**Search Abstract**

In recent years surveys have identified several dozen B stars in the Milky Way halo moving faster than the local escape speed. The origin of most of these hypervelocity stars (HVSs) is still poorly constrained. Here we show that the velocity distribution, and in particular the deficiency in >700 km/s HVSs is inconsistent with binary disruptions by the massive black hole (MBH) in the Galactic Centre. This conclusion holds in the full and empty loss cone regime, and for secular instabilities in eccentric disks. Accounting for multiple close encounters between binaries and the MBH, does not qualitatively change the results. Moreover, there is no observed counterpart population in the Galactic Centre that is consistent with the HVSs. The star-formation history could be tuned explain the HVS velocity distribution, but this tuning would produce a mismatch with the observed HVS flight times. Frequent stellar collisions of the binary components due to interactions with the MBH do not significantly impact the velocity distribution in the Galactic halo. Such collisions, however, can leave observable remnants in the Galactic Centre, and potentially explain the origins of G2-like dust clouds.

**DeepXiv brief**

[research paper] This study performs the most comprehensive prediction to date of the kinematic and astrophysical properties of runaway helium-rich stars ejected from single-degenerate Type Ia supernova progenitor systems, using 6 million simulated ejection trajectories via the SHyRT stellar kinematics framework. It identifies two new candidate objects with extreme astrometric properties, finds that higher-mass (>0.7 M☉) ejected companions are overrepresented in observable space, and shows that current observations support a Galactic SN Ia rate ~3–2×10⁻⁶ yr⁻¹—three orders of magnitude lower than inferred from SN Ia rates—suggesting the single-degenerate channel is not dominant, yet a large fraction of predicted runaway stars remain undetected, implying a previously underestimated contribution to Type Ia SNe.

**Search Abstract**
